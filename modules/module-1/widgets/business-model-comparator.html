üèüÔ∏è Sports Business Model

Product & Competition

Product
Uncertain outcome entertainment - fans don't know who will win
Competition
Competitors are partners - you need strong opponents to create exciting product
League Structure
Cartel model with revenue sharing to maintain competitive balance

Customer Relationship

Loyalty
Extreme brand loyalty - multi-generational, emotional connection
Switching Costs
Very high - fans rarely change teams (identity, community, tradition)
Customer Lifetime Value
Decades-long relationships from childhood through retirement

Revenue Model

Direct Sales
Tickets, merchandise - but only ~40% of total revenue
B2B Revenue
Sponsorship, media rights dominate - selling attention/reach to other businesses
Pricing Power
Premium pricing in top markets due to scarcity and loyalty

Supply & Demand

Inventory
Fixed supply - can't add more home games if demand increases
Perishability
Tickets expire at game time - unsold seats = lost revenue forever
Simultaneity
Production and consumption happen simultaneously - can't stockpile

Market Dynamics

Barriers to Entry
Extremely high - leagues control franchise rights, limited expansion
Geographic Monopoly
One team per market in most cases - protected territories
Content Value
Live sports = must-watch TV - can't be time-shifted without losing value

üè™ Traditional Consumer Business

Product & Competition

Product
Predictable, standardized offering - customer knows what they're getting
Competition
Competitors are threats - goal is to eliminate or outperform rivals
Market Structure
Independent businesses competing for market share

Customer Relationship

Loyalty
Moderate brand loyalty - driven by quality, price, and convenience
Switching Costs
Low to moderate - customers will switch for better value/quality
Customer Lifetime Value
Variable - depends on category (consumables vs durables)

Revenue Model

Direct Sales
Primary revenue from selling products/services to consumers
B2B Revenue
Minimal - may exist in wholesale channels but not core model
Pricing Power
Constrained by competition and price sensitivity

Supply & Demand

Inventory
Flexible supply - can increase production to meet demand
Perishability
Most products can be stored and sold later (except fresh food)
Simultaneity
Production happens before consumption - inventory management

Market Dynamics

Barriers to Entry
Variable - depends on industry (tech vs manufacturing vs retail)
Geographic Monopoly
Rare - usually compete in same markets with multiple brands
Content Value
Value not time-dependent - customers consume when convenient

üîë Key Strategic Implications

1. Interdependence Paradox

  • Sports: Strong opponents make YOU more valuable
  • Consumer: Weak competitors give you market share
  • Result: Revenue sharing to maintain parity

2. Dual Revenue Model

  • Sports: B2C (fans) AND B2B (sponsors, media)
  • Consumer: Primarily B2C or B2B, not both
  • Result: Must optimize for two different customers

3. Emotional vs Rational

  • Sports: Emotional, identity-driven loyalty
  • Consumer: Rational choice based on value
  • Result: Higher price tolerance, lifetime value

4. Supply Constraints

  • Sports: Can't scale inventory (82 games is 82 games)
  • Consumer: Scale production to meet demand
  • Result: Scarcity pricing, dynamic pricing

5. Content Perishability

  • Sports: Value disappears after game (spoilers)
  • Consumer: Products retain value over time
  • Result: Premium on live viewing/attendance

6. Competitive Balance

  • Sports: League enforces parity (draft, salary cap)
  • Consumer: Winner-take-all dynamics are fine
  • Result: Every team needs chance to win
